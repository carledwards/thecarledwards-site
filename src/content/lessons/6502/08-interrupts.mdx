Lesson 7 ended with the CPU stuck in `wait:` asking the timer "done
yet?" over and over. That's *polling* — simple, but the CPU can't do
anything else while it asks. An **interrupt** flips it around: the timer
yanks the CPU's attention the instant it fires. The CPU finishes its
current instruction, jumps to a handler you wrote (the **ISR** —
interrupt service routine), and when the ISR runs `RTI` it picks up
exactly where it left off, as if nothing happened.

To make that work you point the 6502's **IRQ vector** (`$FFFE`) at your
ISR, enable the timer's interrupt in the VIA (`ViaIER`), and `CLI` to
let interrupts through. The main program is then free to do *nothing* —
or anything — while the ISR advances the LEDs on the timer's beat.

This is the first time you'll reach past "just instructions" and tell
the assembler where something goes — which is exactly when that extra
control earns its keep.

Once running, set a **break-on-interrupt** and CodeLab stops the moment
control transfers — you can single-step *into* the ISR and watch the
return address sitting on the stack. Seeing the hand-off is the lesson.
